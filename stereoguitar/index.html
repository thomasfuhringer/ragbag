Stereo Guitar 2.0

I modified a Telecaster guitar to output a stereo signal.
It plays the lower three strings on the left stereo channel and the treble strings on the right.
That way it sounds somewhat like sitting in front of the piano in that you hear the lower notes more to your left and the higher ones to the right.
The real point, however, is to run the two channels through a separate signal chain and thus account for the specific properties of the strings and frequency range and musical function of each voice register.

To get that, I used a 'split blade' pickup, which has two coils in line. They are both connected but I tapped the signal between the two coils and run it out through a stereo jack.
I put in a second tone pot so I can set a cutoff frequency (dial in a tone) for each of the channels. Also the volume pot needed to be replaced with a stereo one.
To make it possible to switch back to mono, I swapped the pickup selector for a five-way 'Megaswitch', which allows for a lot of combinations.

I did the same with this Precision Bass. In this case it was simpler because it already comes with split coil pickups. All I had to do was tap the wire between the two parts of the pickup and run it out through a stereo jack.
I replaced one pot with a push-pull pot to switch between mono and stereo. Pushed in, the instrument behaves as it came from the factory.

In the case of a bass there might be less of a point in separating the signal because you do not play those arpeggios across the fretboard, which bring the stereo signal to shine. But there might still be value in having a separate signal chain for the upper and lower two strings, e.g. to shape a two-part tone for slapping.
